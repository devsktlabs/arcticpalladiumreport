Palladium Demand Beyond Automotive: Industrial and Investment Uses

By Chris Moller · 2026-03-23

palladiumindustrial demandelectronicsinvestment

While automotive catalytic converters account for approximately 70-75% of total palladium demand, the remaining 25-30% comes from industrial applications and investment demand that provides diversification and partially offsets the long-term automotive demand decline driven by EV adoption.

Electronics: Multi-Layer Ceramic Capacitors

Palladium is used in multi-layer ceramic capacitors (MLCCs), which are the most common type of capacitor in electronic devices:

  • Application: MLCCs are used in virtually every electronic device, including smartphones, computers, automotive electronics, medical devices, and industrial equipment
  • Palladium's role: Palladium (often as palladium-silver alloy) is used as the internal electrode material in MLCCs due to its excellent conductivity, corrosion resistance, and compatibility with ceramic dielectric materials
  • Demand scale: Approximately 1.0-1.5 million ounces of palladium annually are consumed in electronics
  • Trend: Growing demand driven by the proliferation of electronic devices, 5G infrastructure, IoT, and electric vehicles (EVs themselves contain more electronics and therefore more MLCCs)

Importantly, EVs actually increase palladium demand from electronics even as they eliminate catalytic converter demand. Each EV contains more electronic components than a comparable ICE vehicle.

Chemical Industry Catalysts

Palladium catalysts are essential in several chemical manufacturing processes:

  • Nitric acid production: Palladium-containing gauzes are used in the Ostwald process for ammonia oxidation to nitric acid. Nitric acid is a primary industrial chemical used in fertilizer production
  • Acetic acid production: Palladium catalysts are used in the Cativa process for acetic acid production from methanol. Acetic acid is used in plastics, textiles, and food additives
  • Fine chemicals and pharmaceuticals: Palladium catalysts are used in the synthesis of numerous fine chemicals and pharmaceutical intermediates
  • Petrochemical refining: Palladium catalysts are used in petroleum refining processes including hydrogenation and dehydrogenation

Chemical industry palladium demand is relatively stable and grows with global industrial production.

Dental Applications

Palladium alloys are used in dental applications:

  • Dental crowns and bridges: Palladium-gold-silver alloys are used for dental restorations due to their biocompatibility, corrosion resistance, and strength
  • Trend: Dental palladium demand has declined as ceramic and zirconia alternatives have become more popular, but palladium alloys remain in use for certain applications
  • Demand scale: Approximately 100,000-200,000 ounces annually (declining)

Jewelry

Palladium is increasingly used in jewelry, particularly in China:

  • White gold alternative: Palladium provides a natural white color (unlike white gold, which is yellow gold alloyed with nickel or palladium and plated with rhodium)
  • Lightweight: Palladium is lighter than platinum, making it comfortable for larger jewelry pieces
  • Chinese market: Chinese consumers are the primary market for palladium jewelry, where it is marketed as a premium white metal
  • Demand scale: Approximately 200,000-400,000 ounces annually
  • Trend: Growing in China, stable elsewhere

Investment Demand

Palladium investment demand includes bars, coins, ETFs, and other financial instruments:

  • Palladium coins: Canadian Palladium Maple Leaf, Russian Ballerina, and other coins provide investment-grade palladium
  • ETFs: ETFS Physical Palladium Shares (PALL) and other ETFs hold physical palladium on behalf of investors
  • Bar and coin investment: Approximately 300,000-600,000 ounces annually, depending on price environment
  • Price sensitivity: Investment demand is highly price-sensitive. When prices rise, investment demand typically increases (momentum buying). When prices fall, investment demand typically decreases

Hydrogen Economy: Future Palladium Demand?

Palladium has potential applications in the hydrogen economy, though platinum is the dominant PGM in fuel cells:

  • Hydrogen sensors: Palladium-based sensors are used for hydrogen detection in fuel cell vehicles, hydrogen storage facilities, and industrial applications
  • Hydrogen purification: Palladium membranes can be used to purify hydrogen to the high purity required by fuel cells
  • Fuel cell research: Some research explores palladium alloy catalysts as alternatives to pure platinum in fuel cells

These applications are small today but could grow if the hydrogen economy develops.

The Non-Automotive Demand Outlook

Non-automotive palladium demand is projected to grow modestly:

  • Electronics: Growing, driven by electronic device proliferation and EV electronics content
  • Chemical catalysts: Growing with global industrial production
  • Dental: Declining as alternative materials gain market share
  • Jewelry: Growing in China, stable elsewhere
  • Investment: Variable, dependent on price environment and investor sentiment

Total non-automotive demand of approximately 2.0-2.5 million ounces annually provides a base level of demand that will persist regardless of the automotive transition.

Implications for Skaergaard

Non-automotive palladium demand is relevant to Skaergaard in several ways:

  • Demand floor: Non-automotive demand of 2.0-2.5M oz/yr provides a base demand level that persists even if automotive demand declines. This base demand supports palladium prices
  • Electronics-EV paradox: EVs reduce catalytic converter palladium demand but increase electronics palladium demand. The net effect is still negative for total palladium demand, but less negative than considering catalytic converters alone
  • Industrial diversification: Skaergaard's palladium production serves non-automotive demand as well as automotive demand, providing some diversification against automotive-specific disruptions

Conclusion

Non-automotive palladium demand provides important diversification that partially offsets the long-term automotive demand decline. Electronics demand, in particular, is growing and benefits from the same technology trends (electrification, connectivity) that are reducing automotive palladium demand. For investors, understanding the non-automotive demand components provides a more complete picture of the long-term palladium market outlook and Skaergaard's position within it.
