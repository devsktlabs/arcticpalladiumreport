Palladium Mining Methods: Open Pit vs Underground at Skaergaard

By Chris Moller · 2026-03-23

palladiummining methodsopen pitundergroundskaergaard

The choice of mining method is one of the most consequential decisions in mine development. For Skaergaard, the primary mining method is planned as open-pit, with potential for underground mining in later years. This article examines how the mining method choice affects project economics and risk.

Open-Pit Mining at Skaergaard

Open-pit mining is the planned primary method for Skaergaard, driven by the near-surface nature of the PGM-gold mineralization:

  • **Geological suitability**: The mineralized zones at Skaergaard are close to surface, making open-pit mining the logical choice for the upper portions of the deposit
  • **Cost advantage**: Open-pit mining is significantly cheaper than underground mining. Operating costs of US$2-5 per tonne of ore (versus US$15-30 for underground) translate to lower AISC per ounce
  • **Scale**: Open-pit mines can process larger tonnages per day than underground mines, supporting higher annual production
  • **Recovery**: Open-pit mining achieves near-complete ore recovery within the pit design, unlike underground methods that may leave pillars or unrecovered ore

    • Open-pit challenges at Skaergaard:

  • **Stripping ratio**: The ratio of waste rock to ore must be favorable. If the mineralized zones are deep beneath overburden, the stripping ratio increases, raising costs
  • **Pit slope stability**: Arctic conditions (permafrost, freeze-thaw cycles) can affect pit wall stability, requiring conservative pit designs
  • **Blast optimization**: Cold temperatures affect explosive performance, requiring modified blast designs
  • **Seasonal productivity**: Winter conditions (extreme cold, snow, limited daylight) reduce open-pit productivity

    • Underground Mining Potential

    Below the planned open-pit, Skaergaard's mineralization may continue to depth, creating potential for underground mining:

  • **Extended mine life**: Underground mining below the open-pit could extend the mine life by 10-20 years
  • **Higher-grade ore**: In many deposits, grades improve at depth as the ore body narrows and mineralization becomes more concentrated
  • **Technical complexity**: Underground mining in an Arctic environment adds complexity (ground freezing, ventilation heating, underground water management)
  • **Higher costs**: Underground mining costs are 3-6 times higher per tonne than open-pit

    • Underground mining methods that could be considered for Skaergaard:

  • **Sub-level caving**: A mass mining method suitable for large, steeply dipping ore bodies. High production rates but significant surface subsidence
  • **Longhole stoping**: Suitable for tabular or steeply dipping ore zones. Moderate production rates with good selectivity
  • **Room and pillar**: Suitable for flatter ore zones. Lower production rates but good ground control

    • The specific underground method would be determined during the feasibility study based on the geometry and geotechnical characteristics of the deeper mineralization.

    Comparative Economics

    The economic difference between open-pit and underground mining is significant:

  • **Open-pit AISC**: Typically US$800-1,200/oz PdEq for a deposit like Skaergaard (after byproduct credits)
  • **Underground AISC**: Typically US$1,200-1,800/oz PdEq for the same deposit, reflecting higher per-tonne costs

    • The transition from open-pit to underground represents a step-change in operating costs. Project economics are typically strongest during the open-pit phase and weaker during the underground phase.

    Mine Life Implications

    The mining method choice directly affects mine life:

  • **Open-pit only**: Skaergaard's open-pit might support 15-25 years of production depending on pit depth, stripping ratio, and processing rate
  • **Open-pit + underground**: Adding underground mining could extend total mine life to 25-40+ years
  • **Processing rate**: The processing plant capacity (tonnes per day) also affects mine life. A larger plant processes ore faster, shortening mine life but increasing annual production

    • Geotechnical Considerations in the Arctic

    Mining in an Arctic environment creates specific geotechnical challenges:

  • **Permafrost**: Frozen ground affects pit slope stability, foundation design, and waste rock dump stability. Thawing permafrost can cause ground subsidence and increased water flow
  • **Freeze-thaw cycles**: Repeated freezing and thawing of pit walls can weaken rock mass and cause rockfall
  • **Ice lenses**: Ground ice can cause unexpected ground conditions during pit development
  • **Water management**: Seasonal snowmelt and permafrost thaw create variable groundwater conditions that must be managed

    • These factors require conservative pit design, comprehensive geotechnical monitoring, and adaptive mine planning.

    Mining Sequence Optimization

    The optimal mining sequence maximizes net present value by:

  • **Mining higher-grade ore early**: Processing higher-grade zones in the early years generates more revenue and reduces payback period
  • **Minimizing pre-stripping**: The amount of waste that must be removed before accessing ore affects the timing of revenue generation
  • **Balancing cut-off grade**: The cut-off grade may vary over the mine life to optimize the grade-tonnage trade-off at each stage
  • **Phased development**: Phasing pit development (mining one pit phase at a time) reduces upfront capital requirements

    • Conclusion

    Skaergaard's open-pit mining plan provides a significant cost advantage over underground alternatives, with estimated AISC 30-50% lower than what underground mining would produce. The open-pit phase is expected to support 15-25 years of production, with underground mining potentially extending mine life further. For investors, the open-pit mining method is a material positive for Skaergaard's near-term economics, while the underground potential provides optionality for extending mine life and resource utilization.
