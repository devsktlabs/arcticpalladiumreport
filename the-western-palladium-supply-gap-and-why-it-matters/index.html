The Western Palladium Supply Gap and Why It Matters

By Chris Moller · 2026-03-23

palladiumsupply gapwestern supplycritical mineral

The Western world has a palladium problem. Approximately 85-90% of global palladium supply originates from just two countries: South Africa and Russia. Neither is a reliable long-term supply partner for Western nations. South Africa faces deep structural decline in its PGM sector, and Russia is a geopolitical adversary subject to Western sanctions. Closing the Western palladium supply gap is a strategic imperative for automotive, defense, and energy transition industries.

The Scale of the Gap

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

To understand the Western supply gap, consider palladium supply by geopolitical alignment:

  • Western-aligned supply: USA (Stillwater Complex, ~300,000 oz/yr Pd), Canada (~100,000 oz/yr Pd from Sudbury and other operations), Zimbabwe (uncertain alignment, ~300,000 oz/yr Pd), recycling (~2.0-2.5M oz/yr, geographically distributed)
  • Non-Western supply: South Africa (~3.5-4.0M oz/yr Pd), Russia (~600,000-800,000 oz/yr Pd)
  • Total supply: ~7.5-8.0M oz/yr
  • Western share: Approximately 30-35% of total supply (including recycling)

Western industries consume more palladium than Western-aligned sources produce, creating a structural supply gap that must be filled by imports from South Africa and Russia. This dependency creates strategic vulnerability.

Why the Gap Is Widening

The Western supply gap is not stable. It is widening for several reasons:

  • SA production declining: South African PGM production is falling by 1-3% per year as mines deepen and grades decline
  • Russian supply constrained: Sanctions limit Norilsk's operations and Western market access
  • Recycling not growing fast enough: Recycling growth is limited by vehicle fleet turnover rates
  • Western mines depleting: Stillwater's reserves are finite, and Canadian PGM production is small
  • No major new Western projects: Very few new PGM projects are being developed in Western-aligned jurisdictions

Without new Western supply, the gap will widen as SA production declines and demand (even with EV-driven reduction) remains substantial.

The Automotive Vulnerability

The automotive sector is most exposed to the palladium supply gap:

  • European automakers: BMW, Mercedes-Benz, Volkswagen, and other European manufacturers depend on palladium for catalytic converters. Supply disruption would halt vehicle production
  • Japanese automakers: Toyota, Honda, and other Japanese manufacturers are similarly dependent
  • US automakers: GM, Ford, and Stellantis use significant palladium in their vehicles
  • Transition timeline: EV adoption is reducing the automotive palladium demand growth rate, but the existing ICE vehicle fleet will continue to require PGMs for 15-20 more years

Defense Implications

Palladium is used in defense applications:

  • Military vehicle catalytic converters: Military vehicles, including tactical vehicles and armored personnel carriers, use catalytic converters that require PGMs
  • Chemical catalysts: PGM catalysts are used in the production of explosives and propellants
  • Electronics: Palladium is used in military-grade electronics and communications equipment
  • Strategic reserves: Some Western nations include palladium in strategic stockpiles, but reserves are limited

The Skaergaard Solution

Skaergaard directly addresses the Western supply gap:

  • New Western supply: Greenland (Denmark, NATO, EU-associated) is unambiguously Western-aligned
  • Scale: If Skaergaard produces 200,000-400,000 oz/yr Pd, it would represent 3-5% of global supply and 10-15% of current Western-aligned primary supply
  • Gold hedge: The gold byproduct reduces the dependence on palladium prices alone for project viability
  • Timing: Potential production in the early 2030s coincides with the projected widening of the supply gap

Skaergaard is not large enough to close the Western supply gap alone, but it is the most significant new Western PGM project currently in development.

Other Potential Solutions

Beyond Skaergaard, several approaches could narrow the gap:

  • Stillwater expansion: Increasing production from the only existing US PGM mine, though reserve limitations constrain the scale of expansion
  • Canadian exploration: Exploration for PGM deposits in the Canadian Shield and other prospective regions
  • Platinum substitution: Increasing platinum content in catalytic converters reduces palladium demand, but platinum supply is also concentrated (70%+ in South Africa)
  • Hydrogen fuel cells: Long-term substitution of fuel cells (which use platinum) for ICE engines would shift demand from palladium to platinum, but this is a 20-30 year transition
  • Improved recycling: Increasing catalytic converter collection and recovery rates in emerging markets

Conclusion

The Western palladium supply gap is a strategic vulnerability that is widening as South African production declines and Russian supply remains constrained. Skaergaard offers the most significant near-term new Western supply contribution, though it alone cannot close the gap. For investors, the strategic imperative to diversify PGM supply away from SA and Russia creates a durable tailwind for Western PGM projects like Skaergaard. The supply gap is not just a market opportunity; it is a geopolitical necessity.
