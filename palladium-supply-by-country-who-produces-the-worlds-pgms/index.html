Palladium Supply by Country: Who Produces the World's PGMs

By Chris Moller · 2026-03-23

palladium supplyproduction by countrysouth africarussia

Understanding where the world's palladium comes from is the first step to understanding the market's risk profile and the strategic value of new supply sources. This article provides a country-by-country breakdown of global palladium production.

South Africa: The Dominant Producer

  • **Share of global supply**: Approximately 70-75% of mine production (approximately 3.5-4.0 million ounces palladium per year)
  • **Key deposits**: Bushveld Complex (Merensky Reef, UG2 Reef, Platreef)
  • **Major producers**: Anglo American Platinum, Impala Platinum, Sibanye-Stillwater, Northam Platinum
  • **Trend**: Production in structural decline due to deepening mines, declining grades, energy crisis, and operational challenges

    • South Africa's dominance in palladium production is both a geological and historical phenomenon. The Bushveld Complex contains approximately 75-80% of the world's known PGM resources. Mining has been ongoing since the 1920s, and South Africa has built a mature PGM industry with deep expertise and established infrastructure.

    Russia: The Second Source

  • **Share of global supply**: Approximately 10-12% (approximately 600,000-800,000 ounces palladium per year)
  • **Key deposits**: Norilsk-Talnakh deposits on the Taimyr Peninsula
  • **Major producer**: Nornickel (Norilsk Nickel)
  • **Trend**: Production constrained by sanctions, market access difficulties, and operational challenges

    • Russia's palladium production comes from Norilsk, one of the world's largest and most complex mining operations. Norilsk's PGMs are produced as byproducts of nickel-copper mining, meaning PGM supply is tied to nickel and copper production economics. Western sanctions since 2022 have constrained Norilsk's access to technology, equipment, and markets, affecting production efficiency.

    United States: The Only Major Western Producer

  • **Share of global supply**: Approximately 3-5% (approximately 250,000-350,000 ounces palladium per year)
  • **Key deposits**: Stillwater Complex (J-M Reef), Montana
  • **Major producer**: Sibanye-Stillwater (Stillwater and East Boulder operations)
  • **Trend**: Production relatively stable but reserve-constrained; limited growth potential

    • The Stillwater Complex is the only primary PGM mine in the United States and one of only two significant PGM mines in the Western Hemisphere. Its production is important for US domestic supply but represents a small fraction of global supply. The Stillwater operation is an underground mine with challenging geology, constraining production growth.

    Canada: Small but Strategic

  • **Share of global supply**: Approximately 1-3% (approximately 80,000-150,000 ounces palladium per year)
  • **Key deposits**: Sudbury Basin (Ontario), various smaller deposits
  • **Major producers**: Vale (Sudbury), Glencore (Sudbury), various junior explorers
  • **Trend**: Production stable but small; significant exploration potential in the Canadian Shield

    • Canadian palladium production is primarily a byproduct of nickel-copper mining in the Sudbury Basin. The Sudbury operations are among the world's largest nickel-copper mining districts, but palladium is a relatively small contributor to total metal value. Several exploration companies are exploring for primary PGM deposits in Canada, but none have reached production.

    Zimbabwe: Growing but Politically Risky

  • **Share of global supply**: Approximately 3-5% (approximately 250,000-350,000 ounces palladium per year)
  • **Key deposits**: Great Dyke (Main Sulfide Zone)
  • **Major producers**: Zimplats (Implats), Mimosa (Implats/Sibanye), Unki (Amplats)
  • **Trend**: Growing as South African producers shift investment to Zimbabwe's shallower, lower-cost operations

    • Zimbabwe's Great Dyke is the world's second-largest PGM-bearing layered intrusion after the Bushveld. Zimbabwean operations have lower mining costs than South Africa (shallower ore, lower energy costs), but the jurisdiction carries significant political and sovereign risk.

    Other Producers

    Several other countries produce small amounts of palladium:

  • **Australia**: Minor production from nickel-copper operations in Western Australia
  • **Finland**: Small production from Sotka and other PGM occurrences
  • **Colombia**: Minor PGM production from alluvial deposits
  • **Various**: Small amounts from recycling operations in Japan, Europe, and North America

    • The Supply Diversification Problem

    The production breakdown reveals a critical concentration:

  • **South Africa + Russia**: 80-87% of global mine supply
  • **Western-aligned supply**: Less than 15% of global mine supply (USA + Canada + recycling)
  • **Other**: Less than 5%

    • This concentration is extreme by commodity standards. For comparison, copper supply is distributed across approximately 20 major producing countries, and gold supply comes from approximately 40 countries.

    Where New Supply Could Come From

    New palladium supply could come from several sources:

  • **Skaergaard (Greenland)**: The most significant new Western-aligned PGM project currently in development. Potential production of 200,000-400,000 oz/yr Pd
  • **Ivanhoe Platreef (South Africa)**: Large but still in South Africa, so does not solve jurisdictional concentration. Potential production of 400,000-600,000 oz/yr Pd
  • **Canadian exploration**: Several junior companies exploring for PGM deposits in the Canadian Shield
  • **Recycling increase**: Higher collection rates and improved recovery technology could increase recycled supply
  • **Existing mine expansions**: Expanding Stillwater, Sudbury, or other existing operations

    • Conclusion

    Global palladium supply is dangerously concentrated in South Africa and Russia, creating strategic vulnerability for Western nations. The USA (Stillwater) and Canada (Sudbury) provide only a small fraction of Western demand. Skaergaard in Greenland represents the most significant new Western-aligned palladium supply project, directly addressing the supply concentration problem. For investors, the country-by-country supply breakdown underscores the strategic value of Skaergaard's jurisdictional diversification.
