Palladium in Catalytic Converters: How It Works and Why It Matters

By Chris Moller · 2026-03-23

palladiumcatalytic converterautomotiveemissions

Palladium's dominant use is in automotive catalytic converters, where it serves as a catalyst to reduce harmful exhaust emissions. Understanding how palladium works in this application, and how the automotive transition affects demand, is essential for evaluating the long-term PGM market outlook.

The Emissions Problem

Internal combustion engines produce several harmful exhaust gases:

  • Carbon monoxide (CO): A poisonous gas produced by incomplete combustion
  • Hydrocarbons (HC): Unburned or partially burned fuel, contributing to smog
  • Nitrogen oxides (NOx): Produced when nitrogen and oxygen in the air react at high temperatures. NOx contributes to smog, acid rain, and respiratory problems

Catalytic converters use precious metal catalysts to convert these harmful gases into less harmful substances:

  • CO is converted to carbon dioxide (CO2)
  • HC is converted to carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O)
  • NOx is converted to nitrogen (N2) and oxygen (O2)

How Three-Way Catalytic Converters Work

Modern three-way catalytic converters (TWCs) simultaneously reduce all three pollutant categories. The TWC contains a ceramic monolith coated with a "washcoat" that holds the precious metal catalysts:

  • Palladium: The primary catalyst for oxidation reactions. Palladium catalyzes the conversion of CO to CO2 and HC to CO2 + H2O (oxidation catalyst)
  • Rhodium: The primary catalyst for NOx reduction. Rhodium catalyzes the conversion of NOx to N2 + O2 (reduction catalyst). Rhodium is particularly effective for this reaction and is difficult to substitute
  • Platinum: Used as a secondary oxidation catalyst and as a substitute for palladium in some formulations. Platinum also provides oxygen storage capability that helps maintain the optimal air-fuel ratio for catalytic conversion

Gasoline vs Diesel Catalytic Converters

The catalyst formulation differs between gasoline and diesel engines:

  • Gasoline engines: Traditionally used palladium-rich formulations for oxidation (CO and HC conversion) and small amounts of rhodium for NOx reduction. Typical PGM loading: 3-7 grams per converter
  • Diesel engines: Used platinum-rich formulations because diesel exhaust has a higher oxygen content, requiring different catalyst chemistry. Also require NOx aftertreatment ( SCR, LNT) that uses additional PGMs

The diesel-to-gasoline vehicle shift (especially in Europe post-dieselgate) shifted demand from platinum to palladium, contributing to the palladium price premium over platinum in 2018-2021.

PGM Loading Trends

Automakers have progressively reduced PGM loading per vehicle:

  • 2000: Typical PGM loading of 5-10 grams per converter
  • 2010: Reduced to 3-7 grams through improved catalyst formulations and more efficient engine designs
  • 2020: Further reduced to 2-5 grams through advanced washcoat technology, improved oxygen storage, and engine optimization
  • 2030 (projected): Continued reduction to 1-4 grams through further technology improvements

This trend means that each new vehicle uses less palladium than in the past, partially offsetting the growth in vehicle production.

The EV Transition and Palladium Demand

The shift to electric vehicles is the most significant long-term threat to palladium demand:

  • Battery electric vehicles (BEVs): No catalytic converter required. Zero PGM demand for emissions control
  • Plug-in hybrids (PHEVs): Use catalytic converters for the gasoline engine component, but PGM loading may be lower than for conventional ICE vehicles due to smaller engine size
  • Fuel cell vehicles (FCEVs): Use platinum (not palladium) in the fuel cell. Create platinum demand but not palladium demand

Demand trajectory:

  • 2026: EVs (BEV + PHEV) represent approximately 20-25% of new vehicle sales globally. The remaining 75-80% of new vehicles use catalytic converters with palladium
  • 2030: EVs projected to represent 35-45% of new vehicle sales. Palladium demand from new vehicles declining but not collapsing
  • 2035: EVs projected to represent 50-60% of new vehicle sales. Palladium demand from new vehicles significantly reduced
  • 2040+: EVs projected to represent 70%+ of new vehicle sales. Automotive palladium demand substantially diminished

The Fleet Effect

The existing vehicle fleet provides a buffer against demand decline:

  • Global fleet: Approximately 1.4 billion vehicles on the road, the vast majority with catalytic converters
  • Replacement rate: Approximately 70-80 million vehicles are scrapped annually worldwide
  • Time lag: Palladium demand from new vehicle production will decline before palladium demand from the existing fleet declines, because it takes 15-20 years for the global fleet to turn over
  • Recycling: As ICE vehicles are scrapped, their PGMs are recycled, but recycled PGMs primarily offset new supply rather than creating new demand

Implications for Skaergaard

The catalytic converter story has several implications for Skaergaard:

  • Near-term demand: Palladium demand remains strong for the next 10-15 years as the fleet transitions. Skaergaard's potential production timeline (early 2030s) aligns with the period when automotive palladium demand begins to meaningfully decline
  • Gold hedge: The gold byproduct credit provides a hedge against palladium demand decline. If automotive palladium demand falls faster than expected, gold revenue provides a floor for project economics
  • Rhodium buffer: Rhodium demand for NOx reduction is less affected by EV adoption (hybrids still need NOx control), providing additional revenue diversification
  • Long-term uncertainty: By the time Skaergaard is in its second decade of production (2040s), automotive palladium demand may be substantially reduced. Long-life mine economics depend on non-automotive demand growth or higher prices

Conclusion

Palladium's role in catalytic converters has been the primary driver of demand for decades. The EV transition is a genuine long-term demand challenge, but the fleet effect provides a 15-20 year buffer. Skaergaard's multi-metal profile (Pd + Au + Rh) provides diversification that pure palladium investments cannot match. For investors, the catalytic converter demand trajectory should be modeled carefully, with particular attention to the timing of Skaergaard's production relative to the EV adoption curve.
