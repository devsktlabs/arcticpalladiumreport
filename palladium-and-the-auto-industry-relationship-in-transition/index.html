Palladium and the Auto Industry: A Relationship in Transition

By Chris Moller · 2026-03-23

palladiumauto industryevscatalytic converters

For over three decades, the automotive industry has been palladium's dominant customer. The relationship has been mutually beneficial: palladium enables automakers to meet increasingly stringent emissions standards, and automotive demand has driven palladium prices to levels that support mining investment. Now, the electric vehicle transition is fundamentally reshaping this relationship.

The Peak of Automotive Palladium Demand

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

Automotive palladium demand peaked in the late 2010s and early 2020s:

  • 2018-2021: Record palladium prices above US$2,500/oz reflected extreme automotive demand driven by the diesel-to-gasoline shift in Europe and tightening emissions standards globally
  • Peak demand: Approximately 6.0-6.5 million ounces of palladium annually were consumed by the automotive sector at peak
  • Drivers: Stricter emissions standards (Euro 6, China VI), diesel-to-gasoline shift (dieselgate), and growing vehicle production in China and India

The Transition Begins

The EV transition is now the dominant force shaping automotive palladium demand:

  • 2026 EV market share: BEVs and PHEVs represent approximately 20-25% of new vehicle sales globally
  • Regional variation: EV market share ranges from over 50% in Norway to under 5% in India and Southeast Asia
  • Accelerating adoption: EV adoption is accelerating, with most major automakers committing to EV-dominant lineups by 2030-2035

The Demand Trajectory

Automotive palladium demand is projected to decline, but the trajectory is gradual rather than cliff-like:

  • 2026: Approximately 5.0-5.5 million ounces of automotive palladium demand
  • 2030: Approximately 4.0-4.5 million ounces (declining as EV share reaches 35-45%)
  • 2035: Approximately 2.5-3.5 million ounces (declining as EV share reaches 50-60%)
  • 2040: Approximately 1.5-2.5 million ounces (declining as EV share reaches 70%+)

The gradual decline reflects the slow turnover of the global vehicle fleet. Even as new vehicle sales shift to EVs, the existing fleet of over 1 billion ICE vehicles continues to require catalytic converter replacements and spare parts.

Automaker Responses

Automakers are adapting their PGM strategies:

  • Palladium-to-platinum substitution: Many automakers are reformulating catalytic converters to use more platinum and less palladium. The substitution rate depends on the Pd-Pt price ratio
  • PGM loading reduction: Advanced catalyst technology allows automakers to maintain emissions compliance with less PGM per vehicle
  • Hybrid strategy: PHEVs continue to use catalytic converters, and hybridization is growing faster than full BEV adoption in some markets
  • Fuel cell development: Some automakers are developing hydrogen fuel cell vehicles (which use platinum, not palladium), creating future platinum demand but not palladium demand

What This Means for Skaergaard

The automotive transition affects Skaergaard in several important ways:

  • Near-term (2026-2035): Automotive palladium demand remains substantial, supporting palladium prices at levels that make Skaergaard viable. The first 10 years of production (if Skaergaard begins production in the early 2030s) coincide with the period of most significant automotive demand
  • Medium-term (2035-2045): Automotive palladium demand decline accelerates. Skaergaard's economics in this period depend increasingly on gold revenue and non-automotive palladium demand
  • Gold hedge: The gold byproduct credit becomes increasingly important as automotive palladium demand declines. In later mine years, Skaergaard may effectively be a gold mine with palladium credits rather than a palladium mine with gold credits

The Hybrid Wildcard

Plug-in hybrid vehicles (PHEVs) are a wildcard in the demand forecast:

  • PHEVs use catalytic converters: Unlike BEVs, PHEVs have internal combustion engines and require catalytic converters
  • PHEV growth is strong: In many markets, PHEV sales are growing faster than BEV sales due to range anxiety, charging infrastructure gaps, and lower cost
  • PHEV loading: PHEVs may require less PGM per vehicle than conventional ICE vehicles due to smaller engine size, but the overall demand contribution is positive

If PHEV adoption remains strong for longer than currently projected, the automotive palladium demand decline would be less severe.

Non-Automotive Demand Growth

Non-automotive palladium demand provides a partial offset:

  • Electronics: Growing demand from MLCCs, partially driven by EV electronics content
  • Chemical catalysts: Stable demand from industrial chemical production
  • Hydrogen applications: Growing demand from hydrogen sensors and purification membranes
  • Investment: Variable but provides a price floor during demand transitions

Conclusion

The relationship between palladium and the automotive industry is in transition, but it is not ending overnight. Automotive palladium demand will remain substantial for the next 10-15 years, providing a favorable window for Skaergaard's development and early production years. The gold byproduct credit becomes increasingly important as automotive palladium demand declines. For investors, the key question is not whether automotive palladium demand will decline (it will), but how quickly, and whether gold credits can compensate for the gradual palladium demand reduction.
