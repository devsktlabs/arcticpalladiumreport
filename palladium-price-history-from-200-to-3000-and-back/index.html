Palladium Price History: From $200 to $3,000 and Back

By Chris Moller · 2026-03-23

palladiumprice historycommoditiesmarket cycles

Palladium has been one of the most volatile precious metals of the past three decades. From under US$200 per ounce in the 1990s to over US$3,000 in 2021, palladium's price history is a story of supply scarcity, automotive demand shifts, and market thinness. Understanding these cycles provides context for evaluating the current price environment and future outlook.

The 1990s: The Ford Catalyst Spike

In the late 1990s, palladium prices began a dramatic rise from around US$150/oz to over US$1,000/oz by early 2001. The primary driver was Ford Motor Company's decision to use palladium-rich three-way catalytic converters across its vehicle fleet:

  • **Ford's bet**: Ford committed to palladium-based catalyst technology, creating massive demand
  • **Russian supply disruption**: Russia (then the world's largest palladium producer) temporarily restricted exports for geopolitical reasons, creating a supply shock
  • **Market panic**: The combination of surging demand and constrained supply drove prices to unprecedented levels
  • **Ford's reversal**: By 2001, Ford had switched back to less palladium-intensive catalyst formulations, contributing to a price crash
  • **Aftermath**: Prices fell from over US$1,000/oz to approximately US$200/oz by 2003, devastating investors and mining companies that had bet on high prices

    • The Ford episode demonstrated the vulnerability of the palladium market to demand shifts from a single automaker and the consequences of concentrated supply.

    The 2003-2008 Recovery and Financial Crisis

    Palladium prices recovered gradually from the 2001 crash:

  • **2003-2007**: Prices rose from approximately US$200/oz to over US$500/oz, driven by recovering automotive demand and tightening emission standards
  • **2008 financial crisis**: Prices crashed from approximately US$500/oz to under US$200/oz as global demand collapsed
  • **Recovery**: Prices recovered to approximately US$400/oz by early 2010

    • The 2008 crash demonstrated palladium's sensitivity to macroeconomic conditions. As an industrial precious metal, palladium is hit hard by economic downturns.

    2010-2017: The Gradual Rise

    The period from 2010 to 2017 saw a sustained rise in palladium prices from approximately US$400/oz to approximately US$1,000/oz:

  • **Dieselgate**: The 2015 Volkswagen emissions scandal accelerated the shift from diesel (platinum-intensive) to gasoline (palladium-intensive) vehicles in Europe, boosting palladium demand
  • **SA supply concerns**: Ongoing production challenges in South Africa (labor unrest, deepening mines) constrained supply
  • **Chinese demand growth**: Rapid growth in Chinese vehicle production and ownership drove increasing palladium consumption
  • **Investment demand**: Growing investor interest in palladium as a commodity investment added demand

    • 2018-2021: The Historic Rally

    The most dramatic phase of palladium's price history saw prices surge from approximately US$1,000/oz in early 2018 to over US$3,000/oz in May 2021:

  • **Severe supply deficit**: South African production declined due to COVID-19 lockdowns, energy crisis, and structural issues
  • **Diesel-to-gasoline shift**: Continued shift from diesel to gasoline vehicles globally, especially in Europe and India
  • **Substitution limits**: Despite high prices, automakers were slow to substitute platinum for palladium due to engineering lead times
  • **Market thinness**: With only ~8 million ounces of annual supply, the palladium market is thin. Even small supply-demand imbalances cause large price movements
  • **Investor buying**: Speculative and strategic buying by investors amplified the price rise
  • **Russian supply concerns**: Geopolitical tensions (pre-Ukraine invasion) created concerns about Russian supply reliability

    • 2022-2026: The Correction and Stabilization

    After the 2021 peak, palladium entered a significant correction:

  • **Demand concerns**: Growing EV adoption raised concerns about long-term palladium demand destruction
  • **Platinum substitution**: Automakers accelerated palladium-to-platinum substitution at the high Pd-Pt price ratio
  • **SA supply recovery**: South African production recovered partially from COVID-19 disruptions
  • **Macro tightening**: Central bank interest rate increases reduced investment demand for precious metals
  • **Ukraine impact**: The 2022 Russian invasion initially boosted prices (supply concern) but ultimately weighed on prices (demand destruction from economic slowdown)
  • **2026 stabilization**: Prices have stabilized in the US$1,800 (Feb 2026)-1,000/oz range as the market finds a new equilibrium

    • Lessons for Investors

    Several lessons emerge from palladium's price history:

  • **Expect volatility**: Palladium is inherently volatile due to the thin market and supply concentration. Investors should be prepared for large price swings
  • **Supply shocks matter**: The palladium market is highly sensitive to supply disruptions from South Africa or Russia, which can cause rapid price spikes
  • **Demand shifts can be sudden**: Automaker decisions about catalyst technology can rapidly shift demand
  • **Substitution is real but slow**: Platinum substitution acts as a price ceiling for palladium, but the substitution process takes years
  • **Don't extrapolate**: The 2021 peak above US$3,000/oz proved unsustainable. Extreme prices tend to correct

    • Current Price Context

    At US$1,800 (Feb 2026)-1,000/oz in 2026, palladium is trading below its long-term average (approximately US$800-1,200/oz adjusted for inflation). This price level is:

  • **Above SA marginal cost**: Supportive of continued SA production
  • **Below substitution trigger**: Below the ratio where significant platinum substitution is triggered
  • **Near breakeven for marginal projects**: New PGM projects need prices at or above this level for viable economics

    • Conclusion

    Palladium's price history is defined by extreme volatility, driven by the thin market, supply concentration, and automotive demand dynamics. The current price environment at US$1,800 (Feb 2026)-1,000/oz represents a post-correction stabilization that balances constrained supply against evolving demand. For investors, palladium's volatility means that project economics should be tested across a range of price scenarios, with particular attention to the downside risk of lower prices during demand transitions.
